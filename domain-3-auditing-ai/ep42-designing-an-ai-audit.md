# Designing an AI Audit: Objectives, Scoping, and Resources

Evaluating complex automated systems demands a comprehensive evaluation strategy that goes far beyond a traditional information technology review. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series walks through how assessing artificial intelligence fundamentally differs from conventional IT audits, the exact steps to define the boundaries of your assessment, the core objectives you must achieve, and how to assemble the right resources. Mastering this framework moves you from a simple compliance checker to a strategic organizational protector — able to pinpoint hidden biases that could lead to massive discrimination lawsuits and advise leadership on whether a shiny new technology truly aligns with the long-term goals of the business or is just a costly distraction.

## Why the Scope Expands for Intelligent Systems

In a traditional information systems audit, you are typically looking at predictable logic, much like inspecting the mechanical components of an elevator: you can see the cables, trace the electrical signals from the buttons, and know exactly why the car goes up or down. If a user presses a button, a specific, pre-programmed action occurs.

Assessing advanced algorithms requires an entirely different mindset and a specialized vocabulary. You are now dealing with **machine learning**, where computers find patterns without explicit programming, and **natural language processing**, which lets machines interpret human speech and text. Because these technologies learn and adapt, your scope must widen significantly. You must ask whether the historical information feeding the system is reliable and free from prejudice, whether the final decisions can actually be explained to a human being, and whether the system's actions could introduce new dangers or cause harm to individuals.

## The Black Box Challenge

This brings us to a major hurdle: the sheer technical depth and complexity involved. Many modern applications rely on **deep learning**, which uses complicated statistics and multiple layers of artificial neural networks that process data in opaque ways. *Opaque* means the internal decision-making process acts like a black box. Think of a master baker who creates a legendary loaf of bread but cannot give you a precise recipe because they adjust the flour and water by instinct, based on the humidity of the room and the feel of the dough. Deep learning operates similarly — the exact path the machine took is hidden below the surface, making it incredibly difficult to interpret and prove the decision was based on safe and diverse data. Overcoming this requires blending deep technical expertise with sharp professional judgment.

## A Top-Down, Holistic Design Strategy

When you sit down to design your evaluation plan, you must begin with a **top-down approach** — starting with the broader strategic goals of the entire enterprise before you ever look at a single line of code. You must be a holistic thinker, ensuring a tactical technological deployment does not accidentally destroy the company's future objectives.

Your design phase must therefore prioritize trustworthiness, ethical behavior, and privacy. You are no longer just checking whether the software is efficient, secure, and operational; you are assessing the overarching governance and accountability structures and focusing on long-term impacts. Consider a company deploying an automated customer service chatbot. A narrow technical review might just confirm the bot answers within two seconds and does not crash the server. But a holistic evaluation asks whether the bot might accidentally discriminate against customers with non-native accents over time, leading to a public relations disaster, reputational harm, and regulatory fines down the road. You must exercise independence and strategic foresight to evaluate the true longevity and sustainability of the investment.

## The Six Core Objectives

To achieve this strategic vision, there are six overarching objectives you must meet when reviewing the development and management processes of these intelligent tools:

1. **Consistent, integrated approach** — verify the organization builds solutions consistently and that this approach aligns with the larger goals of the enterprise, avoiding isolated teams building rogue projects.
2. **Design and outcomes alignment** — ensure both the tool's design and its intended outcomes move in the exact same direction as the company's strategy.
3. **Money and management** — confirm standard enterprise project management practices are used and that financial reporting and controls are strictly followed.
4. **System development life cycle** — review the step-by-step process of planning, creating, testing, and deploying the software, ensuring every station on that "assembly line" is overseen effectively and transparently.
5. **Legal and regulatory compliance** — validate that the entire project complies with all current legal and regulatory requirements.
6. **Continuous communication** — ensure there is indicative operational reporting so clear data on performance, ethical behavior, and outcomes is regularly sent to the accountable human owner and the governance committee.

## Resourcing the Audit and the Six-Step Scoping Process

Because this field evolves at a blistering pace, you will often evaluate technologies brand new to you. It is entirely expected and encouraged to bring in highly skilled staff or external advisors — a practice known as **co-sourcing**. These subject matter experts provide the deep technical insights you need to accurately draw the boundaries of your assessment and determine your testing methodologies.

When defining the exact scope, follow this six-step process:

1. **Identification** — precisely determine which systems are under review and explicitly identify the human or department accountable for them.
2. **Data** — decide exactly which datasets will be accessed and tested, then formally obtain permissions.
3. **Documentation** — find out where the architectural, development logs, and testing reports are stored and acquire access.
4. **Review history** — examine assessments already conducted, including prior privacy and security tests, second-line testing results (the risk and compliance teams who double-check front-line developers), and any prior trustworthy or ethical assessments.
5. **Regulatory mapping** — ensure every applicable technology law and regulation is accounted for during planning so nothing slips through the cracks.
6. **Specialized documentation** — acquire the paperwork unique to intelligent systems: transparency and explainability documentation that explains the algorithmic decision-making process, plus reports detailing the outcomes of bias and ethics testing.

## Key Takeaways

* Auditing AI requires widening scope beyond predictable IT logic to question data reliability, decision transparency, and potential harm.
* Deep learning often acts as an opaque black box, so auditors must blend technical expertise with professional judgment to interpret decisions.
* Design the audit top-down and holistically, prioritizing trustworthiness, ethics, privacy, governance, and long-term business impacts over mere efficiency.
* Six core objectives cover integrated alignment, design-outcome alignment, project and financial management, the SDLC, legal compliance, and operational reporting.
* Use co-sourcing for unfamiliar technologies and follow a six-step scoping process from identification through acquiring specialized explainability and bias documentation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does auditing AI differ from a traditional IT audit?
A traditional IT audit examines predictable logic, like inspecting an elevator where pressing a button triggers a specific pre-programmed action. AI systems learn and adapt, so the scope must widen to ask whether the historical data is reliable and free from prejudice, whether decisions are transparent and explainable, and whether the system could cause harm. Deep learning often acts as an opaque black box, making the decision path hidden below the surface.

### What is a top-down approach when designing an AI audit?
A top-down approach means starting by looking at the broader strategic goals of the entire enterprise before examining a single line of code. The auditor must be a holistic thinker who ensures a tactical technological deployment does not accidentally destroy the company's future objectives, prioritizing trustworthiness, ethical behavior, privacy, governance, and long-term impacts rather than just efficiency and security.

### What are the six core objectives of an AI audit?
The six objectives are to verify a consistent integrated approach aligned with enterprise goals, ensure the design and intended outcomes match company strategy, confirm standard project management and financial controls, examine the system development life cycle for transparent oversight, validate compliance with all legal and regulatory requirements, and ensure indicative operational reporting flows to the accountable owner and governance committee.

### What is the six-step process for scoping an AI audit?
Step one is identification of the systems and accountable owner. Step two is deciding which datasets to access and obtaining permissions. Step three is locating documentation like architectural and testing reports. Step four is reviewing prior privacy, security, second-line, and ethical assessments. Step five is regulatory mapping of all applicable laws. Step six is acquiring specialized transparency, explainability, bias, and ethics documentation unique to intelligent systems.
