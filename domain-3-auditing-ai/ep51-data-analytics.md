# AI Data Analytics: Sentiment, Trend, and Anomaly Detection

Automated tools let auditors examine massive amounts of information to uncover actionable facts and even understand human emotions. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series explains how to shift away from manual checking and instead use intelligent systems to measure how successful a new technology rollout really is. Mastering these concepts lets you confidently advise management on whether a new automated workflow is actually working or causing silent frustration among employees — pointing to hard data and emotional trends to guide corporate strategy instead of guessing based on a few watercooler conversations.

## What Is Data Analytics?

At its simplest, data analytics is the practice of asking precise questions of your data to get factual, actionable answers. In the audit world, we sometimes call this the **interrogation of data**. This process uses a variety of software tools, both free and paid, along with specific techniques to examine information.

For an AI auditor, reliance on data analytics is growing rapidly. Think about a traditional manual audit, where an auditor might spend weeks looking through thousands of individual employee travel receipts to find duplicates or checking hundreds of system login times to spot security breaches. It is exactly like a teacher manually grading thousands of multiple-choice tests by hand — slow, repetitive, and prone to human error.

Data analytics eliminates those manual chores. Artificial intelligence can automate the review of thousands of records in a matter of seconds, acting like an intelligent scanner that instantly flags the anomalies for you to look at. When auditors train AI models to execute this testing and even automate the creation of final reports, it fundamentally changes the role of the auditor. Instead of being buried in paperwork, you are freed up to focus on high-level strategy and complex project auditing.

## Sentiment Analysis and Polarity

A very specific and powerful type of data analytics is **sentiment analysis**, which you might also hear referred to as **opinion mining**. Sentiment analysis is the process of scanning large volumes of text to determine the underlying emotional tone, with the goal of classifying that text as expressing a positive, negative, or neutral opinion. In the technical world, this classification of emotional direction is called **polarity**.

Imagine you run a massive city transit system and you just launched a new mobile ticketing application. Instead of having a human read fifty thousand application store reviews to see if people like it, you use sentiment analysis. The system reads all the reviews and tells you the overall mood of your commuters. Businesses use this constantly to monitor social media trends and measure public opinion about their brand.

## Sentiment Analysis as an Audit Validation Tool

For an AI auditor, sentiment analysis is a brilliant tool for validation. If your company deploys a brand-new artificial intelligence tool for the customer service team, you need to know if it is actually helping. You can run sentiment analysis on the internal feedback surveys, which gives you concrete evidence to validate the success of the AI deployment and gauge exactly what the stakeholders think about it. This turns soft impressions into hard, defensible data that supports your audit conclusions.

## Rule-Based vs. Machine Learning Sentiment Analysis

There are two main types of sentiment analysis you need to understand for the exam.

The first is **rule-based sentiment analysis**, the simpler method. The system is trained to simply hunt for specific keywords in a block of text. If it sees words like *wonderful* or *fast*, it scores the text as positive; if it sees words like *broken* or *awful*, it scores it as negative. It is basically a giant game of keyword matching.

The second type is **machine learning sentiment analysis**, which is far more sophisticated. Instead of just looking for isolated keywords, the system analyzes the words *and the specific order in which they appear* to understand context. For example, if a customer writes that the new update was "a total lack of improvement," a simple rule-based system might see the word *improvement* and think it is positive — but a machine learning system understands word order and correctly flags the phrase as negative.

### The Algorithms Behind Machine Learning Sentiment Analysis

The machine learning approach relies on specific mathematical algorithms, and you must know their names:

* **Linear regression** — a statistical way to track trends between variables.
* **Naive Bayes** — calculates the probability of an emotion based on past data.
* **Support vector machines** — draw complex mathematical boundaries to sort data points.
* **Deep learning** — uses artificial neural networks to mimic human brain processing.

You do not need to program these algorithms yourself, but you must recognize them as the engines that power advanced opinion mining.
