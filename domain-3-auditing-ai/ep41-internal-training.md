# Internal Training for AI Auditors: Knowledge and Practical Skills

Building internal competency to audit advanced algorithmic systems is a deliberate, structured effort. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series covers the foundational knowledge, specific technical areas, and practical skills your team needs to evaluate modern technologies effectively. By mastering these concepts you will be able to step confidently into any procurement meeting, evaluate whether a vendor's new smart application is secure enough for your company's data, and ask the right questions — ensuring your organization avoids the legal and reputational disasters that come from deploying unchecked or biased algorithms.

## Start with a Shared Definition of AI

Before you can train an audit team, the entire organization must agree on a single definition of what artificial intelligence actually means for the business. You might adopt a definition from an established framework like the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) or a major regulation like the European Union AI Act.

The most crucial distinction to understand is that these systems are **not programmed in the traditional sense**. Traditional software follows a rigid set of rules provided by a human. AI systems are designed to be trained using data. Think of traditional programming like baking a cake using a strict recipe — add two cups of flour, bake at a specific temperature, and get the exact same cake every time. Training a modern algorithm is more like teaching a child to recognize different types of animals: you show them hundreds of pictures of cats and dogs, correcting them when wrong, until they learn the visual patterns on their own.

## The Hierarchy and Scope of AI Capabilities

Since the surge in public awareness following the release of consumer chatbots in late 2022, many people use technical terms interchangeably, but an auditor must be precise. The hierarchy nests like this:

* **Artificial Intelligence** is the broadest category — any advanced computer system that can simulate human capabilities based on predetermined rules.
* **Machine Learning** sits inside AI: a specific type of program that builds a predictive model by feeding on input data.
* **Deep Learning** sits deep within machine learning, using highly complex, layered structures to process information, similar to how human brain cells connect.

Imagine a massive transportation system: AI is the entire concept of moving people from place to place, machine learning is specifically the train networks within that system, and deep learning is the high-speed bullet train operating on the most advanced tracks.

On the scope of capability, **Artificial Narrow Intelligence** is a system restricted to one highly specific area of knowledge — and every commercial system in use today falls into this category. It is like a world-class pastry chef who makes incredible desserts but cannot fix your car engine. By contrast, **Artificial General Intelligence** is a theoretical concept: a system capable of any intellectual task a human can perform, including emotional engagement, fine motor skills, and creative problem-solving — a super-mechanic who is simultaneously a world-class chef, a surgeon, and a composer.

## The Ten Critical Knowledge Areas

To audit these environments, professionals must develop a multidisciplinary skill set spanning ten specific areas:

1. **Technical knowledge** — foundational concepts like natural language processing (how a computer reads and generates text) and computer vision (how a machine interprets images).
2. **Basic concepts and terminology** — especially how they align with industry standards and regulations.
3. **Common models and applications** — you do not need to build them, but you must recognize the commercially available tools your business is likely to purchase.
4. **Bias** — because these systems excel at finding patterns, they often accidentally learn human prejudices hidden in the data. Bias is usually an unintentional design flaw, like a resume screener that only selects candidates from a specific university because historical data featured that school. The algorithm is not malicious, but its outcome is discriminatory, so auditors must test outcomes.
5. **Governance** — the overarching framework of policies, management oversight, and controls that holds the organization accountable.
6. **Ethics** — divided into applied ethics (fairness, privacy, and human rights in everyday operations) and theoretical ethics (deeper moral dilemmas like consequentialism, which judges morality by the final outcome). Theoretical ethics is the philosophical debate about building a dam; applied ethics is ensuring the water flow does not unfairly flood a neighborhood.
7. **Trustworthy design** — ensuring technologies are transparent, secure, and reliable, prioritizing human welfare above all to prevent harm.
8. **Specialized risk assessment frameworks** — these tools introduce entirely new hazards, like the risk of discriminatory harm from bad training data, rarely considered when auditing traditional accounting software.
9. **Audit-specific applications** — knowing how to use smart tools to make your own auditing faster and more accurate.
10. **Regulatory and compliance knowledge** — staying updated on the laws governing algorithmic use specific to your region and industry.

## Practical Skills Development

Bridging the gap between theory and actual auditing requires practical skills development. Because this field requires knowledge of information technology, data science, and business strategy, it is a highly collaborative effort. Auditors must exercise professional judgment to decide which tests apply to specific environments.

To build this muscle, **continuous education is mandatory** — the technology evolves too fast for annual, static training. Organizations should heavily utilize case studies and real-world scenarios. Even more effective are **simulation exercises**. Think of a simulation like a corporate fire drill: you create a hypothetical scenario where a major algorithm goes rogue, and cross-functional teams must collaborate to assess the damage and audit the failure in real time. If an organization lacks the internal expertise to run these simulations, bringing in external experts is a highly effective way to close knowledge gaps and elevate the entire enterprise.

## Key Takeaways

* The whole organization must agree on a single AI definition and recognize that AI is trained via data, not rigidly programmed like traditional software.
* The hierarchy nests AI, then machine learning, then deep learning; current commercial systems are all narrow intelligence, while general intelligence remains theoretical.
* Ten knowledge areas span technical knowledge, terminology, common models, bias, governance, ethics, trustworthy design, risk frameworks, audit applications, and regulatory compliance.
* Bias is usually an unintentional flaw learned from historical data, so auditors must test outcomes rather than assume malice.
* Practical skills come from continuous education, case studies, and cross-functional simulation exercises, supplemented by external experts when internal expertise is lacking.
