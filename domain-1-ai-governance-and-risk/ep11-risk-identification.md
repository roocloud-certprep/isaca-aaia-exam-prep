# AI Risk Identification: Threat Landscape and Risk Categories

Before any organization can defend against the dangers of artificial intelligence, it must learn to spot them. Episode 11 of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series explores **AI risk identification** — how to spot dangers in AI systems, who the potential attackers are, the specific types of harm these systems can cause, and the unique hurdles we face managing these risks. As an audit professional, this lets you confidently assess whether a company's new AI tool is safe for daily operations or hides unseen dangers, and guide leadership on adoption by accurately mapping what could go wrong before a financial or reputational disaster occurs.

## Integrating AI Risk Identification and Professional Skepticism

AI risk identification is not a one-time event — it must be woven directly into the company's overall risk management program. You have to continuously look for risks on an annual basis, whenever systems change, or when you hire new vendors, accounting for the unique characteristics of AI, including new types of attackers and unique operational challenges.

A major part of this involves addressing human psychology. People naturally tend to trust machines too much, assuming that an AI solution is inherently objective, flawless, and smarter than traditional software. Because of this blind trust, auditors and risk managers must apply **professional skepticism** — bringing a questioning mind and a critical assessment to the evidence rather than taking things at face value. It is like using a car navigation system: just because the digital voice confidently tells you to turn right, you do not blindly steer into a lake. You still look through the windshield and verify the road is actually there.

## The Four Threat Actors

The AI threat landscape is vast and involves many actors trying to exploit these powerful computing systems, usually to abuse the technology or use it as a weapon. We categorize threat actors into four main groups.

* **Insider threats** — an organization's own employees who cause harm, either on purpose or by accident. An accident might be pasting confidential client data into a public chatbot to format an email faster; an intentional act might be siphoning off sensitive financial records to sell them. Like a trusted chef who might leave the secret recipe on a park bench or deliberately steal it.
* **Nation states** — entire countries using AI to advance global strategies through cyberespionage, warfare, or extreme surveillance, such as facial recognition to monitor citizens and suppress their rights — a massive, invisible digital border patrol watching every move from the sky.
* **Cybercriminals** — hackers who use AI to upgrade their attacks, writing perfectly convincing scam emails or coding advanced viruses. Like a burglar who suddenly gets a magical skeleton key that automatically guesses the combination to any lock in the neighborhood.
* **AI developers themselves** — sometimes the people building these tools fail to align them with human ethics, creating a biased algorithm that unfairly denies housing loans to specific groups. Like a sports referee trained to enforce the rules for only one team.

## Three Categories of Harm

AI poses both technological and human-related dangers, and identifying them is famously difficult because these systems are often a mystery — we cannot easily explain how they arrive at their answers. The National Institute of Standards and Technology divides harm into three categories.

* **Harm to people** — can be individual (threatening a person's physical safety or civil liberties), group (discriminating against a specific demographic), or societal (disrupting democratic elections or ruining access to education). Picture it as the difference between wrongfully confiscating one person's transit pass, refusing to sell passes to anyone from a neighborhood, and shutting down the entire city's transit grid.
* **Harm to an organization** — disrupting daily business operations, causing massive financial loss through security breaches, or destroying the company's public reputation. Imagine a factory where machines stop, thieves steal inventory, and the disaster makes front-page news so customers flee.
* **Harm to an ecosystem** — damaging interconnected global systems like the supply chain, the international financial market, or the planet's natural resources. Like poisoning a major river: the poison kills the fish, ruins the fishing industry, bankrupts the town, and ripples across the regional economy.

## Measuring Risk: Scale, Scope, Optionality, and Regulatory Levels

According to the framework by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), there are three primary criteria for determining risk levels:

* **Scale** — how serious the negative impact is and how likely it is to happen, like the difference between getting a papercut and being struck by lightning.
* **Scope** — how wide the application reaches, meaning how many people are affected, like a broken coffee maker in one apartment versus a complete power outage across a major city.
* **Optionality** — whether people actually have a choice to interact with the AI or if its effects are forced upon them, like choosing to ride a rollercoaster versus being forced to breathe heavily polluted air.

Regulations like the **European Union AI Act** categorize these risks into four distinct levels to dictate how strictly they should be governed: **unacceptable risk** (banned entirely, such as dangerous biometric surveillance or social scoring), **high risk**, **limited risk**, and **minimal risk**. By analogy, unacceptable risk is like selling a car with no brakes (completely illegal), high risk is driving a regular car on the highway (license and strict safety rules), limited risk is riding a bicycle (basic safety gear), and minimal risk is simply going for a walk in the park.

## The Seven-Stage AI Lifecycle

To properly identify risks, we must look at the entire lifecycle of an AI system and the different people involved at each step. The framework highlights seven distinct stages and their corresponding actors:

1. **Plan and design**
2. **Collect and process data** — handled by data collectors and processors
3. **Build and use model** — managed by developers and modelers
4. **Verify and validate**
5. **Deploy** — executed by system integrators
6. **Operate and monitor** — overseen by system operators
7. **Use or impacted by** — involving end users and stakeholders

Picture this lifecycle like building a commercial airplane: the people mining the raw aluminum are completely different from the engineers designing the wings, who differ from the test pilots validating the aircraft, who differ from the passengers ultimately impacted by the flight. Risk can enter the process at any of these specialized stages.

## Six Challenges of Managing AI Risk

Often, the people tasked with managing risk are not actually experts in AI, creating a dangerous knowledge gap. To fix this, organizations must build a strong governance structure that forces developers, risk professionals, and auditors to collaborate. The six specific challenges are:

1. **Third-party solutions** — buying from an outside vendor means inheriting their flaws and toxic data, like buying a pre-baked cake from a stranger without knowing if they used expired milk.
2. **The AI lifecycle** — risks change dramatically depending on the stage; a model in design looks very different from one running live, like a harmless puppy that grows into a poorly trained guard dog.
3. **Availability of reliable metrics** — the industry cannot agree on how to measure trustworthiness, so developers invent self-serving scorecards, like a student writing, taking, and grading their own final exam.
4. **The real-world setting** — testing in a controlled lab proves nothing about reality, like a model boat that survives a calm swimming pool but not a hurricane in the open ocean.
5. **Inscrutability** — advanced models lack explainability, creating massive uncertainty, like a brilliant magic eight-ball welded shut so you can never see how it decides.
6. **The human baseline** — to prove a machine is better or safer, you need a baseline of how well a human does the job, like measuring how long it takes a human to sweep a street before claiming a robot is faster.

## Key Takeaways

* AI risk identification must be woven into the overall risk program and revisited annually, on system changes, and when hiring new vendors, always applying professional skepticism.
* The four threat actors are insider threats, nation states, cybercriminals, and AI developers themselves.
* NIST divides harm into three categories: harm to people (individual, group, societal), harm to an organization, and harm to an ecosystem.
* Risk is measured by the OECD criteria of scale, scope, and optionality, and the EU AI Act sorts systems into unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal risk.
* Risk can enter at any of the seven lifecycle stages, and six challenges — third-party solutions, lifecycle shifts, unreliable metrics, real-world settings, inscrutability, and the human baseline — make management hard.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Who are the four main AI threat actors?
The four threat actors are insider threats, which are an organization's own employees who cause harm on purpose or by accident; nation states, which are countries using AI for cyberespionage, warfare, or surveillance; cybercriminals, which are hackers using AI to make attacks faster and harder to catch; and AI developers themselves, who sometimes fail to align their tools with human ethics and create biased algorithms.

### What are the three NIST categories of AI harm?
The National Institute of Standards and Technology divides harm into three categories: harm to people, which can be individual, group, or societal; harm to an organization, including disrupted operations, financial loss, and reputational damage; and harm to an ecosystem, which damages interconnected global systems like the supply chain, financial markets, or the natural environment.

### What are scale, scope, and optionality in AI risk?
According to the OECD framework, scale looks at how serious the negative impact is and how likely it is to happen, like the difference between a papercut and being struck by lightning. Scope measures how wide the application reaches and how many people are affected, like a broken coffee maker versus a citywide power outage. Optionality evaluates whether people actually have a choice to interact with the AI or if its effects are forced upon them.

### What are the four risk levels in the EU AI Act?
The European Union AI Act categorizes risks into four levels to dictate how strictly they are governed: unacceptable risk, which is banned entirely such as dangerous biometric surveillance or social scoring; high risk; limited risk; and minimal risk. By analogy, unacceptable is like a car with no brakes, high risk is driving on a highway, limited risk is riding a bicycle, and minimal risk is going for a walk in the park.
