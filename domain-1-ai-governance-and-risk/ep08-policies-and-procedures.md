# AI Policies & Procedures: Usage, Development, and Deployment

Every organization adopting artificial intelligence needs a rulebook that keeps the company safe while still allowing innovation to thrive. Episode 8 of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series explores how organizations create and implement **AI policies and procedures** — from the high-level AI policy down to the acceptable use rules that govern everyday employees. Whether you are an auditor, an IT manager, or a department leader, mastering this topic lets you act as a trusted advisor who can confidently say yes to helpful technology while clearly defining the boundaries that prevent costly mistakes, such as accidentally exposing sensitive company data.

## The Foundation: Creating Organizational AI Policies

The foundation of AI governance is creating your organizational policies. A policy is essentially a high-level declaration of your company's rules and culture. When bringing artificial intelligence into the business, the very first step is ensuring your new AI rules perfectly match your broader organizational AI strategy. You do not want your technology team pushing for aggressive AI adoption while your policy mandates extreme caution.

Think of the organizational AI strategy as the destination you want to reach on a road trip, and the policies as the traffic laws you must follow to get there safely. Every company handles these traffic laws differently. Some businesses write a completely separate, brand-new manual just for AI. Others simply weave AI concepts into the current standards and procedures they already have.

Before you sit down to draft a massive new document, examine your existing information technology and security policies for potential integration. You might find that your existing rules about data privacy already cover most of what you need for AI. Regardless of the approach, management must document its official stance on AI in a formal policy and make sure it is known to all applicable stakeholders.

## What Goes Inside the Primary AI Policy

The primary AI policy must establish **authorized and prohibited uses** of AI within the organization. Authorized uses are the specific tasks employees are allowed to use AI for, while prohibited uses are the absolute off-limit activities.

Imagine managing a commercial kitchen. Your policy might authorize chefs to use heavy-duty blenders for making soups but strictly prohibit using them to crush solid ice because it breaks the machine. Similarly, an AI policy might authorize using AI to draft marketing emails but prohibit using it to analyze confidential employee medical records.

The policy must also provide expectations for the **governance** of implemented AI solutions. Governance simply means the system of oversight — detailing who is in charge of monitoring the AI, how it will be maintained, and who is responsible if something goes wrong. To build this structure, organizations should leverage available guidance such as generally accepted frameworks. Frameworks are essentially standardized, industry-approved blueprints created by experts to help manage complex risks.

## The Ripple Effect on Procedures

Establishing a new AI-specific policy creates a ripple effect across the entire business. When you finalize your AI rules, updates to applicable standards, plans, or procedures may result. Procedures are the step-by-step instructions people follow to do their jobs.

If an airline updates its high-level policy to allow passengers to bring pets in the cabin, it cannot just stop there. It has to update the specific procedures for the gate agents, the cleaning crew, and the flight attendants to handle this new reality. In the corporate world, if you implement a new AI policy, your training and awareness plans may also need to be updated to ensure organizational understanding of the AI solutions to be implemented.

## The AI Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

Finally, an AI usage policy should be drafted to complement the main AI policy. Similar to the AI policy, the AI usage policy may be integrated into the organization's general acceptable use policy of information systems. An AI acceptable use policy, or AUP, provides a framework for the ethical and responsible deployment of AI.

Given the rapid advancements in generative AI, organizations should provide clear guidance on AI use that considers a **risk-benefit analysis** — weighing the time saved by using a tool against the potential danger of a data leak.

Think of the acceptable use policy like the rules for a community swimming pool. An effective AI AUP should be simple to understand for general users, with no confusing legal jargon. The pool rules clearly state which diving boards are open, which is like the policy explicitly identifying approved AI solutions. If a software program is not on that approved list, employees cannot touch it.

### Guiding Data Usage and Training
Furthermore, the organization should provide guidance to end users on the acceptable use of the organization's data within these solutions. Can employees upload public marketing brochures? Yes. Can they upload unreleased financial spreadsheets? Absolutely not. Finally, training should be provided to users of AI solutions to ensure they understand the AI acceptable use policy before they ever log in.

## Bringing the Policy Framework Together

A complete AI policy framework has three connected layers. First, your AI policies must always align with the overarching AI strategy of your business, and you should always examine existing security rules before writing new ones. Second, management must formally document authorized and prohibited uses and provide expectations for governance, leveraging accepted frameworks. Third, you must create a clear, simple acceptable use policy that identifies approved AI solutions, guides data usage, and is supported by mandatory training. Together these layers let an organization embrace AI confidently without exposing itself to avoidable harm.

## Key Takeaways

* A policy is a high-level declaration of company rules; procedures are the step-by-step instructions, and a new AI policy ripples down into updated procedures, standards, and training plans.
* AI policies must align with the overarching organizational AI strategy, and you should examine existing IT and security policies for integration before drafting a new manual.
* The primary AI policy must establish authorized and prohibited uses and set governance expectations using generally accepted frameworks.
* An AI acceptable use policy (AUP) provides an ethical, responsible deployment framework, stays free of legal jargon, identifies approved solutions, and weighs a risk-benefit analysis.
* End users need clear guidance on acceptable data usage and must be trained on the AUP before they ever log in.

### What must an AI policy contain?

It must also provide expectations for the governance of implemented AI solutions, detailing who monitors the AI, how it is maintained, and who is responsible if something goes wrong, leveraging generally accepted frameworks.
