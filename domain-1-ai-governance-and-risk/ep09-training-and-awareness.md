# AI Training & Awareness: Talent, Skills, and Competencies

Successfully adopting intelligent tools is as much a human challenge as a technical one. Episode 9 of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series focuses on the human element of technological adoption: how to prepare a corporate workforce for **AI training and awareness**, manage the anxiety that accompanies change, and design effective educational pathways. As an auditor or business leader, these frameworks let you evaluate whether an organization is truly prepared to deploy a new automated system, pinpoint dangerous gaps in change management, and prevent costly employee pushback before an expensive system goes live.

## Grounding the Fear of Job Loss in Real Data

When leadership announces an automation strategy, employees often fear that machines will completely replace their livelihoods, and this skepticism can cause a workforce to reject a solution entirely. To ground the fear in reality, look at the data.

A comprehensive 2023 study by the World Economic Forum analyzed a massive dataset of 673 million global jobs. Employers expect artificial intelligence to disrupt 23 percent of jobs — about 83 million positions — by the year 2027. The disruption hits some industries harder than others: sectors like supply chain, transportation, media, entertainment, and sports will see a higher-than-average impact, while manufacturing, retail, and wholesale will experience a lower-than-average impact.

However, disruption does not purely mean destruction. The same study projects the creation of 69 million entirely new jobs, resulting in a **net employment decrease of only two percent**. Millions of workers will simply transition into new opportunities. To survive this shift, organizations must proactively manage their talent and equip staff with updated skills.

## Talent Management and Job Redesign

An organization must carefully calculate the net impact a new system will have on its people. Many governments provide formal guidance for this transition; for example, the Singaporean government outlines four crucial areas for job redesign that serve as an excellent global standard.

* **Transforming jobs** — look at every daily task and categorize it. Can the task be completely automated, can it be augmented by a machine assisting a human, or must it remain strictly in human hands? In a busy kitchen, a machine might perfectly chop all the onions (automation), a tablet might calculate exact ingredient ratios (augmentation), but the head chef must still taste the final dish (a human task).
* **Charting clear pathways between jobs** — once jobs change, you need a map showing how an employee transitions to a new role, which requires standardizing AI tasks across different job types. If the control panels on heavy vehicles are the same, a worker can easily transition from driving a forklift to operating a backhoe.
* **Clearing barriers to digital transformation** — actively remove roadblocks that make learning difficult and heavily support employees during the technical rollout.
* **Enabling effective communication** — employers and employees must build a shared understanding of why and how human capabilities are being augmented, like a ship captain explaining the destination so everyone rows confidently in the same direction.

Crucially, human resources professionals and training specialists must be involved from the very beginning. You must also consult the **process owners** — the everyday managers and frontline employees who actually perform the physical work. If you do not ask them for input, you will miss vital opportunities for efficiency and ignore hidden workforce concerns.

## Defeating the Six Common Fears

Before teaching an employee which buttons to push, you must systematically address six common fears.

1. **Lack of awareness** of how the technology actually helps. The solution is direct education focused on how the tool enhances daily life and elevates the employee's specific role.
2. **General resistance to change.** Appoint innovation champions, sometimes called power users — early adopters with foundational digital literacy who quietly advocate for the new tool and help skeptical coworkers, taking pressure off the official training department.
3. **Fear that training disrupts business operations.** Use phased rollouts and small-batch training sessions that keep the daily business running as usual.
4. **Poor training design and varying learning capacities.** Ensure learning materials are highly accessible and target precise, specific tasks rather than handing out overwhelming theoretical manuals.
5. **Uncertainty about what happens after training.** If a job is fundamentally changed, management must review remuneration (pay scales) and performance metrics to match the new responsibilities.
6. **The perception that job redesign simply means doing more work.** Continuous, transparent communication before, during, and after the rollout is the only way to secure genuine employee buy-in.

## The Four Categories of an AI Training Curriculum

There are four main categories of knowledge every employee needs before using a new intelligent system.

* **Acceptable use policies** — the corporate rulebook. Just as a company car has strict rules about who can drive it and for what purpose, employees must know exactly what they are permitted to do with company technology.
* **The fundamentals** — knowing the basic vocabulary so staff are not intimidated by high-level jargon.
* **Ethical awareness** — staff must grasp foundational concepts like explainability and bias. Bias means the machine might unfairly favor one demographic group over another based on flawed data, while explainability means you can understand the exact logic of how the machine made its decision. For a bank loan officer, bias would be unfairly denying a loan based on an applicant's neighborhood, while explainability is the ability to produce the exact mathematical formula showing why the loan was approved or denied based on income.
* **Solution-specific considerations** — hands-on training for the exact software platform employees will use. If they are using a generative text tool to draft marketing copy, they need precise training on how to write effective text prompts to get the best possible output from that specific system.

## Turning Skepticism into Advocacy

Adopting new intelligent systems requires an extreme focus on human resources and talent management. You must understand the real data on job disruption and proactively manage fears by mapping out clear career transitions. By using small-batch training, updating compensation metrics, and deploying peer champions, you can turn skepticism into powerful advocacy. Finally, ensure your team is comprehensively trained on acceptable use, basic vocabulary, ethical pitfalls like bias, and the specific rules of the road for your unique company tools.
