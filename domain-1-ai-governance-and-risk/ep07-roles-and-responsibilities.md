# AI Roles & Responsibilities: Governance, Build Teams, and Oversight

When a company brings artificial intelligence into its daily operations, dozens of people share responsibility for making it work safely. Understanding **AI roles and responsibilities** is a fundamental skill for any auditor and a core part of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series. The real-world utility of this knowledge appears the moment a technology project goes off the rails: if an intelligent chatbot hallucinates financial data or a predictive model shows bias, you need to know precisely whose office to walk into to investigate the root cause. Knowing these categories lets you quickly tell whether a failure is a technical glitch from the engineers or an oversight failure from the executive board.

## Not Every Company Needs Every Role

Before diving into specific job titles, establish a ground rule: not every company will have every single one of these roles. The specific jobs an organization creates depend entirely on how large the company is and how it chooses to adopt the technology.

Think of it like acquiring a car. If your company decides to build a custom sports car from scratch, you will need to hire mechanical engineers, aerodynamic specialists, and factory workers. But if you just lease a standard sedan from a local dealership, you do not need any of those factory workers. Similarly, if a business simply buys an off-the-shelf software tool from a third-party vendor, its technology department likely will not need to hire specialized data scientists to build anything. With that ground rule set, the roles divide into four pillars of accountability.

## Pillar 1: Leadership and Strategy

The first major group defines the grand vision, sets the goals, and creates the roadmap for how the organization will adopt intelligent systems. These are the captains steering the ship.

* **Executive Management** — the top-tier leaders. Their primary job is setting the tone at the top, meaning they establish a corporate culture that values responsible technology use. They officially sign off on the overarching strategy and, most importantly, hold the ultimate accountability. If a newly implemented system causes harm to the business or the public, the final blame rests on their shoulders.
* **Chief AI Officer** — the primary commander for technological integration, reporting directly to executive management. Think of them as the dedicated project manager for the entire company's technological leap. Depending on the size of the business, this might be a brand-new standalone job title, or an organization might attach these duties to an existing executive like the Chief Information Officer, asking them to wear two hats.
* **AI Steering Committee** — a formal advisory board made up of leaders from entirely different departments such as finance, marketing, and legal. Because these systems can disrupt every corner of a business, this cross-functional group meets to ensure the technology strategy actually helps each department rather than hindering it. Sometimes a company builds a brand-new committee, but often it simply adds the topic to the agenda of an existing technology or innovation committee.

## Pillar 2: Developmental and Operational

Moving down from leadership, these individuals are usually found within the Information Technology department. If leadership decides where the ship is sailing, this group is the engine room — they physically create, implement, and maintain the solutions.

### IT Development
These are the inventors and builders, including data scientists, software engineers, and specialized researchers. Their job is to write the code and train the models to solve specific business problems. If the company wants a tool to predict inventory shortages, the development team builds it from the ground up.

### IT Operations
Once developers finish building the tool, they hand it over to IT Operations, who act as the mechanics. They take the finished software, deploy it onto the company servers, and ensure it runs smoothly day and night. They maintain the supporting infrastructure so the system does not crash when thousands of people try to use it at the same time.

### Product Management
Product managers act as vital translators. Technical engineers often speak in complex jargon and might build features that are mathematically perfect but incredibly confusing for a normal person to use. The product manager stands between the deep technical staff and the everyday end users, ensuring the final software is highly usable and actually solves the problem it was meant to solve.

## Pillar 3: The Users

The third category covers the people whose daily lives are directly impacted by interacting with the new systems, along with the staff dedicated to supporting them. They are the drivers and passengers of the metaphorical vehicle.

* **End Users** — the individuals actually clicking the buttons and reading the outputs. They can be internal employees using a smart tool to draft emails, or external consumers interacting with a smart recommendation engine on a shopping website.
* **Human Resources** — supports the internal employee end users. When intelligent automation takes over routine tasks, employee job descriptions naturally change. HR must step in to manage job classifications and organize relevant job training programs so employees can learn to work alongside their new digital assistants.
* **Customer Service** — supports the external users. If a customer gets confused by an automated chatbot or feels a smart pricing algorithm treated them unfairly, they will call the help desk. Customer service provides the necessary human support to these customer end users.

## Pillar 4: Governance and Oversight

The final category is the critical safety net. These roles focus on ensuring the organization adheres to ethical principles and meets all compliance requirements — the safety inspectors and traffic cops of the corporate world.

* **Governance Committee** — oversees the big picture. They manage the overarching governance program, write the official policies, and report on program metrics, checking the data to ensure the company is actually obeying its own rules.
* **Risk Management** — every large company has a master plan detailing things that could harm the business. The risk management team evaluates the specific dangers introduced by new algorithms and integrates those concerns into the overarching enterprise risk management planning process.
* **Information Security and Privacy** — the vault guards. Because intelligent models require massive amounts of data to learn, this team ensures the security of the data and information systems and manages all privacy-related concerns throughout the entire life cycle of the solution.
* **Internal Audit** — that is us. Our job is to be the independent inspectors, responsible for assessing technology-related risks as a formal part of the overarching audit plan.

## Why This Map Matters for Auditors

Putting the four pillars together gives auditors a diagnostic map of accountability. Leadership sets the vision and takes the ultimate blame. The developmental group builds and maintains the actual machinery. The users interact with the system and receive vital support from human resources and customer service. And governance provides the ethical and secure boundaries that keep the entire operation safe and legal. When a model fails, this map lets you trace the failure to its source and ask the right people the right questions.

## Key Takeaways

* AI accountability splits into four pillars: leadership and strategy, developmental and operational, users, and governance and oversight.
* Not every company has every role — the jobs created depend on company size and whether the firm builds custom tools or buys off the shelf.
* Executive management holds ultimate accountability, while the Chief AI Officer drives integration and the AI steering committee aligns strategy across departments.
* The development, operations, and product management teams build, deploy, and translate the technology; HR and customer service support internal and external users.
* Governance, risk management, information security, and internal audit form the safety net that keeps AI ethical, secure, and compliant.
