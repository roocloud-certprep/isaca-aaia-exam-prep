# AI Standards and Regulations: NIST, ISO, COBIT, and the EU AI Act

Knowing the rules, frameworks, and regulations that surround artificial intelligence is one of the most practical skills in the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** syllabus. This episode of the AAIA exam prep series explores the ecosystem of guidance that keeps AI safe and compliant. When your company decides to implement a new smart chatbot for customer service or an automated financial forecasting tool, you are the person who must evaluate whether it crosses any legal or ethical lines. Knowing these **AI standards and regulations** lets you confidently give the green light to safe projects and stop risky implementations before they result in heavy fines or public-relations disasters.

## Best Practices: The Foundation

The foundation is **best practices** — the generally accepted right way to do something, even before a strict law forces you to do it. The good news is that we do not throw out everything we already know. Basic **information assurance** principles still apply: information assurance simply means keeping data secure and reliable, the same way you would lock down a traditional database and control exactly who has access. When building an AI system, those exact same security and privacy lifecycles are required, and you treat the AI like any other new software going through its standard software development life cycle.

However, AI introduces totally new quirks that need special attention. Because of these unique challenges, we look to three main areas to figure out what our best practices should be: **academic research, industry standards, and government regulations**. By following best practices, developers build ethical systems that minimize harm and avoid non-compliance penalties. Right now, because global laws are so new and mostly untested in courts, many companies rely on their own **internal standards**, which they build by pulling together research, industry guidelines, and their own corporate experience to fill the legal gaps.

## Academic Research: The Early Warning System

Universities and independent research labs act as the early warning system for the tech world. They run experiments and publish papers that highlight potential problems long before a product hits the market. A university cannot pass a law, but its research heavily influences the lawmakers and industry leaders who do. For example, researchers might study how a program makes decisions to ensure the system can be properly audited, focusing on a concept called **explainability** — the ability to look at an AI output and easily understand exactly how the computer arrived at that answer. It is like a teacher grading a math test who needs to see the student's written steps, not just the final number, so humans can hold the system accountable. Another critical research area is how AI impacts specific community groups; reports might analyze how an automated loan-approval system unfairly rejects applications from minority neighborhoods, which is vital input for governments writing fair, equitable technology policies.

## The Six Key AI Frameworks

Once academic research identifies the issues, industry standard groups step in to create **frameworks** — essentially structured checklists or playbooks that help a company organize its operations and stay compliant. These are usually created by large international or industry-specific organizations supported by governments or the private sector. Here are six critical examples to know.

* **ISO/IEC 23053** — from the International Organization for Standardization, this provides a common language and generic framework for systems using machine learning. (Machine learning is AI where the computer learns from spotting patterns in data rather than being explicitly programmed, like recognizing a dog from hundreds of pictures instead of a biology textbook.)
* **ISO/IEC 42001** — focuses on the management system of information-technology AI, where a management system is the set of rules and daily processes a company uses to achieve its goals. This voluntary standard establishes internal processes for ethical use, transparency, and continuous improvement in AI governance.
* **IEEE 7000-2021** — from the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers, this is deeply focused on the human element, giving developers a process to embed human values, privacy, and fairness directly into system design from the very beginning of the life cycle.
* **OECD AI Principles** — from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, which represents countries across the Americas, Europe, and the Asia-Pacific. These highly influential recommendations center on transparency, inclusiveness, accountability, and sustainability.
* **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** — from the United States' National Institute of Standards and Technology, built to help organizations map, measure, and mitigate the risks of building and using AI systems.
* **Model AI Governance Framework (Singapore)** — developed by Singapore's Personal Data Protection Commission, a practical guide to help organizations deploy AI responsibly in real-world corporate scenarios.

## Laws and Regulations: The Enforcement Layer

The final layer of guidance comes from formal **laws and regulations**, which governments use to actively enforce oversight. Right now, most AI laws are so new that they are completely untested in **litigation** — the formal process of taking legal action or suing someone in a court of law. Because almost no one has sued over these yet, the exact boundaries of the law are still blurry, and many rules remain in the draft phase at national or local state levels. Usually these laws point back to the standard frameworks above and state that failing to follow those guidelines will bring financial penalties.

### Five Key Legal Initiatives

* **EU AI Act (proposed)** — groups AI applications by risk level. An AI that sorts spam email is very low risk, while one controlling hospital medical equipment is very high risk, and the law imposes much stricter rules on higher-risk tools.
* **EU AI Liability Directive (proposed)** — aims to clarify who pays civil damages if an AI system causes physical harm or financial loss, just like a court deciding whether the human driver or the manufacturer pays when a self-driving car crashes.
* **Algorithmic Accountability Act (proposed, United States)** — would force companies to formally assess the impact of their automated decision systems and actively fix any biases or risks they find. Many individual US states and federal agencies already enforce their own local technology guidelines.
* **National AI Act (active, United States)** — designed to push forward AI research and deployment, and importantly it was the legislative driving force that required the creation of the NIST Risk Management Framework.
* **AI Development Plan (China)** — outlines the national strategy for technology growth alongside strict ethical boundaries, working in tandem with the broader Data Security Law enacted in 2021 to ensure heavy regulatory control over how data is processed.

## Key Takeaways

* AI best practices build on traditional information assurance and the software development life cycle, but add ethical checks for AI's unique quirks.
* Best practices come from three sources — academic research, industry standards, and government regulations — and many firms use internal standards to fill legal gaps.
* The six frameworks to know are ISO/IEC 23053, ISO/IEC 42001, IEEE 7000-2021, the OECD AI Principles, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework.
* Five legal initiatives to recognize are the EU AI Act, the EU AI Liability Directive, the US Algorithmic Accountability Act, the US National AI Act, and China's AI Development Plan.
* The EU AI Act categorizes applications by risk and attaches financial penalties, while the US National AI Act drove the creation of the NIST framework.
