# AI Strategy Explained: Build vs. Buy vs. Partner and Value Alignment

How does an organization plan its approach to artificial intelligence, balancing incredible rewards against inevitable risks? In this sixth episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series, we explore AI strategy across national, industry, and corporate levels, the OECD AI Principles, cross-functional strategic planning, the opportunities AI unlocks, and the critical concept of value alignment. As an auditor, this knowledge lets you flag disconnected investments early and determine whether technology actually aligns with the core goals of the enterprise.

## What Is an AI Strategy? The Three Levels

In simple terms, an AI strategy is a master plan for how a group will build, buy, and manage artificial intelligence. These plans happen at three distinct levels, which can be compared to the rules of aviation.

* **National strategies** are where governments establish the primary ethical boundaries and laws for their countries, focusing on national security and aligning technology with the values of their citizens. This is like the aviation authority setting the fundamental safety laws for all airspace.
* **Industry strategies** are where trade organizations and professional bodies take those government laws and translate them into specific, practical standards for their sector, while also encouraging innovation. This is like an association of commercial airlines agreeing on standard pilot training hours and communication protocols.
* **Corporate strategies** are where individual companies figure out how to gain an edge over competitors while ensuring they obey all industry and national rules — like an individual airline deciding which routes to fly and what planes to buy to maximize profit while staying perfectly compliant with safety regulations.

## Ethical Foundations and the OECD AI Principles

No matter the level, an AI strategy must be built on established best practices and ethics. Key ethical pillars include **explainability** (understanding how the AI makes decisions), **fairness** (the system does not discriminate), **transparency** (people know when they are interacting with a machine), and **human centricity** (the tool serves human needs above all else).

A crucial global standard for these concepts is the **OECD AI Principles**, which are divided into two main categories you must remember for the exam.

The first category contains **five values-based principles** for building trustworthy AI:

1. Inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being.
2. Respect for the rule of law, human rights, and democratic values, which inherently includes fairness and privacy.
3. Transparency and explainability.
4. Robustness, security, and safety.
5. Accountability, meaning someone must always answer for the system's actions.

The second category shifts focus to governments, offering **five recommendations** for creating healthy AI ecosystems: investing in research and development; fostering an inclusive ecosystem by making data and computing resources available; shaping a policy environment that allows different systems to interoperate smoothly; building human capacity and preparing the workforce for job market changes; and pursuing international cooperation, since digital tools cross physical borders instantly.

## Writing the Strategy: Executive Support and Cross-Functional Teams

How does a company actually write this strategy? First and foremost, senior management must explicitly approve and champion the effort — without executive support, the strategy will fail. Furthermore, a strategy should never be written solely by the information technology department; you need a **cross-functional team**.

Imagine building a suspension bridge: you do not just ask the steelworkers to plan everything. You need structural engineers, environmental scientists, budget planners, and city officials. Similarly, an AI strategy group often includes representatives from information technology, risk management, legal, operations, and finance, and many companies assign the task to an existing steering committee or innovation board. A vital point for your audit perspective: the people drafting this plan do not need to be deeply technical AI programmers. If a company lacks internal technical expertise, the correct move is to hire outside consultants to guide the cross-functional team.

## The Five Business Opportunities of AI

Organizations go through this complex planning process to capture five massive business opportunities:

1. **Increased innovation** — creating entirely new products and business models that leave competitors behind.
2. **Enriched data analysis** — chewing through massive amounts of data instantly to give executives accurate insights for strategic decisions rather than gut feelings.
3. **Improved customer service** — personalizing user interactions and automating helpdesk tasks, boosting client loyalty.
4. **Automation** — taking over routine tasks to cut operational costs and free employees for complex, creative work.
5. **Enhanced and continuous monitoring** — watching networks and transactions twenty-four/seven, immediately flagging risks and ensuring strict regulatory compliance.

However, there is a major challenge for executive management. Unlocking these benefits requires more than just installing new software; it often forces a company to completely redesign its existing business processes and create entirely new operating models. As the script puts it, if a farm buys a modern tractor, it must change how it plants its fields — it cannot just use the tractor exactly like it used a horse.

## AI Vision, Mission, and Value Alignment

Before diving into the technology, an organization must define its **AI Vision and Mission**, communicating the overarching goal to both employees and the public. For example, the European Union established a clear vision to foster excellence and compete globally, outlining goals to enable AI development, become a thriving market hub, ensure the technology is a force for good, and build leadership in high-impact sectors.

This clear vision paves the way for the final critical concept: **value alignment**. The World Economic Forum defines value alignment as designing AI systems that behave in ways consistent with human values and ethical principles. Think of teaching a child to play a sport — you teach the physical skills, but you must also teach sportsmanship and the rules so they do not hurt anyone. What alignment looks like will differ greatly between countries and corporations, but developers must consider four common elements:

* **Community** — solving real problems, adjusting strategies based on local feedback, and collaborating to understand cultural differences.
* **Ethical Foundations** — respecting universal human values, adhering to human rights declarations, using comprehensive philosophical guidelines, and never generating misinformation.
* **Legal Compliance** — strictly obeying specific regional laws such as data privacy regulations.
* **Operational Strategy** — engaging stakeholders early, constantly monitoring real-world performance, and making sustainability and fairness official metrics for success.

To guarantee value alignment throughout the entire AI life cycle, organizations rely on **four key enablers**: frameworks and guidance to act as foundational rules, human engagement to constantly refine the system, organizational change to ensure corporate culture matches the technology, and audits and assessments that verify the AI is actually following the ethical rules and best practices it was designed to respect.

## Key Takeaways

* AI strategy operates at three levels — national (government laws), industry (sector standards), and corporate (competitive edge within the rules).
* The OECD AI Principles split into five values-based principles for trustworthy AI and five recommendations guiding government policy.
* A strong strategy demands explicit executive support and a cross-functional team spanning IT, risk, legal, operations, and finance — not just IT alone.
* AI presents five opportunities: innovation, enriched data analysis, better customer service, automation, and continuous monitoring, but capturing them requires redesigning business processes.
* Value alignment ensures AI behaves consistently with human values across community, ethics, legal compliance, and operations, supported by frameworks, human engagement, organizational change, and audits.
