# Types of AI Explained: Reactive, Limited Memory, ANI, AGI, and ASI

Artificial intelligence terminology is easy to confuse, yet for anyone preparing for the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** certification, classifying a system correctly is a foundational skill. This opening episode of the AAIA exam prep series unpacks the building blocks of automated intelligence: how AI, machine learning, and deep learning relate to one another, the four functional types of AI, the three capability levels, and the specific technological models you will meet in the field. The payoff is practical, not just academic — when your company proposes buying a new automated tool, this knowledge lets you classify its intelligence level, judge whether it poses a severe data privacy risk or is merely a basic rule engine, ask the right questions during vendor assessments, and design appropriate control frameworks.

## AI vs. Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning

Three terms people constantly mix up — Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning — actually sit inside one another like a set of nesting dolls.

* **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** is the broadest category: any computer system that can mimic human thinking or perform analysis based on a set of rules. Think of AI as the entire concept of motorized transportation, covering everything from a basic motorized scooter to a massive high-speed train.
* **Machine Learning (ML)** is a subcategory of AI where the secret ingredient is data. Instead of programming strict rules, you feed the system historical data so it can build a predictive model, using statistical math to find patterns it then applies to new data. To sort apples from oranges, you don't write rules about colour and shape — you show the machine thousands of pictures until it figures out the difference on its own.
* **Deep Learning** is a highly advanced type of ML that relies on multi-level algorithms processing information in layers, growing more abstract at each step. Looking at a photograph, the first layer might see edges, the next sees shapes, and the final layer recognizes a specific human face. Deep learning is behind today's breakthroughs in language translation and speech understanding.

For an auditor, this hierarchy matters because the deeper you go, the harder it becomes to explain *why* a system reached a decision.

## The 4 Functional Types of AI

We categorize systems by how they function into four distinct types.

### 1. Reactive Machines
The oldest and most basic form. A reactive machine responds to a very specific set of inputs and generates an automatic response, with no memory of the past. A classic arcade pinball machine is the perfect example: the flippers move only when you press the button. It does not remember your last game or learn your playing style — it simply reacts.

### 2. Limited Memory
Where most modern technology sits. These systems hold a static baseline of knowledge but also use dynamic memory to briefly store recent observational data to complete a task. A smart thermostat knows the static layout of a building but dynamically reads the temperature over the last few hours to decide when to run the air conditioning — using those brief recent memories to make a better decision today.

### 3. Theory of Mind
Still in its early stages. Theory of mind means the machine understands that humans have thoughts, ideas, and emotions that drive behaviour, so it can process social interactions and adapt its responses to the user's emotional state. A true theory-of-mind airport kiosk would notice you look stressed and are speaking frantically, soften its tone, and bypass the standard menus to get you immediate help.

### 4. Self-Aware AI
This does not exist yet. It would require a machine not only to understand human emotions but to possess its own consciousness and desires — a mastery of neural networks and deep learning we simply have not achieved.

## The 3 Capability Levels of AI

Beyond function, auditors classify systems by their overall capability.

* **Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI):** Often called "weak intelligence," it operates only within a highly specific domain — incredibly fast and accurate, but without genuine consciousness or broad understanding. An automated warehouse robot uses cameras to scan barcodes, a positioning system to navigate the floor, and an internet connection to sync inventory, and it handles dropped boxes and wet floors flawlessly. Ask that same robot to write a poem and it fails completely. ANI covers all reactive and limited-memory systems.
* **Artificial General Intelligence (AGI):** A machine that can perform *any* intellectual task a human can. Reaching it requires mastering seven traits: sensory perception, fine motor skills, complex problem solving, navigation, natural language understanding, creativity, and social and emotional engagement. We are making strides in language and vision, but fine motor skills and common sense keep AGI out of reach.
* **Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI):** Purely theoretical. It would transcend human capability entirely, solving impossible scientific problems at unbelievable speed and possessing its own consciousness — the kind of intelligence that could radically alter human history.

Every AI system running today is ANI. Recognizing that helps auditors set realistic expectations and never trust a narrow system outside its trained domain.

## Key Technological Models Auditors Encounter

### Generative Models and GANs
Generative systems create entirely new, original content — writing code, painting pictures, composing music, or synthesizing voices — by studying massive training data and identifying the underlying patterns. The most revolutionary architecture is the **Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)**, which pits two neural networks against each other: a *generator* that creates fake data and a *discriminator* that tries to catch the fakes. It is like a chef recreating a secret recipe while a food critic tastes each attempt, until the chef gets so good the critic can no longer tell the replica from the original. Auditors must watch for severe risks here: generating or distributing harmful or biased material can bring legal penalties, and because the machine learns from existing human data, there are major concerns about copyright infringement and intellectual property ownership.

### Agentic AI
The next frontier of digital assistants. Unlike standard automation that just follows a script, agentic systems make autonomous decisions to reach a specific objective. They offer three main benefits: greater workplace specialization (built rapidly for granular workflows), strong capability for innovation and experimentation thanks to their problem-solving judgment, and increased trustworthiness because they are far less likely to hallucinate than standard generative tools. They still need human oversight — a poorly articulated goal will make an agent struggle. You will see agents used across six key areas: customer service, healthcare diagnostics, manufacturing operations, managing complex workflows, calculating financial risk, and supporting sales teams.

### Predictive Models
Predictive systems look backward to see forward, using historical data and ML algorithms to forecast future trends — like a seasoned farmer analyzing decades of soil and weather patterns to predict when to plant. In business they appear in three main applications: predicting when manufacturing equipment will break down, forecasting financial market shifts, and predicting when a customer might cancel a subscription. The audit risk lies in the decisions made from these forecasts: both developers and business users must understand how the model arrived at its prediction, because blindly trusting the output without verifying its factual basis risks catastrophic business errors.

## Key Takeaways

* AI, machine learning, and deep learning nest inside one another — from broad rule-based systems down to layered neural networks.
* The four functional types are reactive machines, limited memory, theory of mind, and self-aware AI; only the first two exist today.
* The three capability levels are ANI (all current AI), AGI (human-equivalent, needs seven traits), and ASI (theoretical superintelligence).
* Generative models (often GANs), agentic AI, and predictive models are the three technological models auditors most often encounter — each with distinct risks.
* Classifying a system's type and capability is the foundation for assessing its privacy risk, asking the right vendor questions, and designing controls.

Reactive machines respond to inputs with no memory, limited-memory systems use recent observational data to complete a task, theory of mind understands human emotions and adapts to them, and self-aware AI possesses its own consciousness and does not yet exist.

### What is the difference between ANI, AGI, and ASI?
Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI) is weak intelligence that operates only within a specific domain and covers all current AI. Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) could perform any intellectual task a human can and would need to master seven traits including sensory perception, fine motor skills, and creativity. Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI) is purely theoretical and would transcend human capability entirely.

### How do AI, machine learning, and deep learning relate to each other?
They sit inside one another like nesting dolls. AI is the broadest category of systems that mimic human thinking, machine learning is a subset that learns patterns from historical data instead of fixed rules, and deep learning is an advanced form of ML that processes information in increasingly abstract layers.

### What are GANs in generative AI?

They train against each other until the generator becomes so good the discriminator can no longer tell the difference, which is how highly realistic synthetic video and audio are produced.
