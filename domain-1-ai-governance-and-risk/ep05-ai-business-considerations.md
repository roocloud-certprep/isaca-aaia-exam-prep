# AI Business Considerations: Use Cases, ROI, Vendors, and Hosting

Before any contract is signed or data is shared, organizations must justify why they are investing in artificial intelligence and weigh the costs against the benefits. In this fifth episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Audit (AAIA)** exam prep series, we dive deep into the business considerations and governance of AI solutions — exploring business cases, cost-benefit analysis and ROI, the critical hosting and vendor decisions, the shared responsibility model, and AI governance. These concepts let you confidently evaluate whether a proposed AI tool makes financial and operational sense for your specific workplace.

## Change Management and the AI Business Case

Whenever an organization brings an AI solution into its environment, it must rely on an established **change management process** — the formal, structured way a company introduces any new technology or workflow to avoid disrupting daily operations. You should not treat the implementation of an AI tool any differently than the rollout of a standard email platform or accounting software; the fundamental steps remain the same. What changes is how you handle the tool *after* implementation, because AI introduces highly unique operational and risk factors that traditional software does not.

Every proposed AI project must start with a thoroughly documented **business case** — essentially a persuasive essay designed to justify spending time and money on a project. To be effective, it must state the exact problem the AI is supposed to solve, explain how the technology will solve it, include a comprehensive financial breakdown of expected costs versus anticipated returns, identify potential hazards and mitigation plans, and provide a step-by-step deployment plan. Documenting these elements ensures technology is chosen on purpose, not just because it is popular. Adopting a tool without a clear need is like buying a massive commercial tractor for a small rooftop garden just because it was on sale — impressive, but a complete mismatch for the actual environment.

## Identifying the Problem and Assessing Technology Maturity

The very first step is identifying the core problem. Companies usually find these problems by forming internal groups like an **innovation steering committee**, by directly asking employees where they struggle, or by bringing in outside consultants. Often the problems revolve around slow operational speeds or the need to cut down on human mistakes.

Once the problem is clearly defined, the search for a matching AI solution begins. During this search, an organization must assess how mature and proven the technology is. Industry experts track technologies on a lifecycle curve that measures how hyped a technology is compared to its actual, practical usefulness. If you choose a well-established, standard cloud-based service — like a common automated text translation tool — the path to success is predictable, with clear costs and proven benefits. On the other hand, if a company decides to build a brand-new, highly experimental neural network from scratch, it takes on a massive amount of uncertainty with no clear roadmap to becoming a functional, profitable reality.

## Cost-Benefit Analysis and Return on Investment

While many software costs are standard, AI brings unique expenses across three categories:

* **Implementation costs** — building the system, buying licenses, and training staff on how to use the new prompts.
* **Operational costs** — ongoing system maintenance, massive amounts of electricity to run the processors, and the high salaries of specialized data scientists.
* **Risk and compliance costs** — spending to protect user privacy and managing the company's public reputation if the system makes a high-profile mistake.

These costs are weighed against expected benefits, which must link back to the original problem. Benefits usually fall into three buckets: **efficiency and automation** (the machine handles repetitive chores so humans focus on high-value thinking), **improved decision making** (choices based on oceans of data rather than gut feelings), and **direct cost reduction** (spending less on manual labor and preventable errors).

On the financial return, recent industry surveys show positive trends: roughly three quarters of surveyed business leaders report a positive financial return from generative tools, over eighty percent got their systems fully operational within just six months, and among those tracking revenue growth tied to the technology, a vast majority saw annual revenue increase by six percent or more. However, set realistic expectations — in the early days, the return might look terrible because upfront costs are high and employees are still awkwardly learning the system. It is like installing solar panels: you start in a deep financial hole, but over time the utility bills vanish and the investment pays off. Remember, too, that not all returns are strictly financial; you must also measure human factors like reduced employee stress or a more pleasant customer experience.

## Hosting: Internal vs. Cloud

A company must decide where the "physical brain" of its AI will live — hosted internally on its own property, or rented in the cloud. **Internal hosting** provides ultimate control: you can customize everything, optimize the machinery for your exact tasks, and easily lock down data security because the information never leaves your building. But the downside is severe — you are limited by the hardware you own, electricity bills skyrocket, and you must hire brilliant technicians to keep servers running.

**Cloud hosting** means renting computer power from a massive tech provider over the internet, delivering enormous flexibility and scalability — if you suddenly need ten times the processing power, you just click a button to upgrade. Initial costs are tiny and you do not fix broken hardware. But cloud hosting carries serious limitations: you lose control over the physical environment, you face **vendor lock-in** (becoming so dependent on one provider that switching becomes financially impossible), and you face **network latency**, a tiny delay as data travels from their servers to your office. Most importantly, your sensitive data leaves your controlled fortress, so the physical geographic location of those external servers becomes a massive compliance issue, since many international privacy laws dictate exactly where citizen data can and cannot travel.

## Vendor Vetting and Service Level Agreements

When bringing in outside vendors, the vetting process is critical. It starts with standard procurement steps to assess general contract risks, but for advanced systems the assessment must dig deeper. You must verify the vendor shares your organization's ethical values and ensure their systems have **explainability** — meaning a human can look under the hood and understand exactly how and why the computer arrived at a conclusion. Without explainability, you are blindly trusting a black box. You must also deeply audit their privacy and security protocols.

Once a vendor is hired, the relationship requires constant attention. The company must establish clear **service level agreements (SLAs)** — legally binding promises about how fast and reliable the system will be — and review them regularly based on the risk level. The reviewers must thoroughly understand their own company's technological goals so they know what red flags to look for, and this ongoing vendor performance data is a crucial part of recalculating ROI over time.

## Off-the-Shelf Solutions and the Shared Responsibility Model

Organizations lacking the programmers to build custom tools often buy **off-the-shelf solutions** — pre-built, ready-to-use applications from an outside company. They are fantastic for speed but introduce a dangerous blurring of boundaries, raising three major risks: **accountability** (if no one documents who maintains the system, it will break and compliance will fail), **ethical concerns** (massive confusion over whether the vendor who built the tool or the bank that bought it is liable when an algorithm makes an unfair decision), and **data security** (sharing private data creates new attack vectors — unprotected doors hackers can try to open).

Crucially, the ultimate responsibility for defining who does what lies entirely with the organization purchasing the tool. The industry standard for this is the **Shared Responsibility Model**. Imagine hiring a catering company: if you rent only their empty kitchen (**Infrastructure as a Service**), you are entirely responsible for the ingredients and cooking safely; if you rent the kitchen plus basic prep staff (**Platform as a Service**), responsibility is shared; and if you order fully prepared meals (**Software as a Service**), they handle the heavy lifting. But even then, you are still responsible for checking IDs at the door and making sure guests behave. In the digital world, that means you are always responsible for **user training, access controls, and governing your own data**, no matter how much underlying tech the vendor handles.

## AI Governance and Program Management

All of this leads to governance and program management. **Governance** refers to the rules, standards, and guardrails a company puts in place to guarantee its advanced tools are used safely and ethically — like traffic laws that exist not to prevent driving but to create predictable lanes and speed limits so everyone reaches their destination without crashing. Many companies try to force AI into their old, traditional IT governance frameworks; while that works as a starting point, it is usually not enough, because intelligent systems behave unpredictably and learn over time, breaking the mold of rigid software.

A strong governance program follows a specific lifecycle: leadership defines the grand strategy, assigns concrete roles and responsibilities to specific humans, writes formal policies and procedures, runs training and awareness campaigns so staff actually understand them, and makes the entire program measurable by tracking specific metrics and reporting them back to an oversight board to prove the guardrails are working.

## Key Takeaways

* Every AI project needs a documented business case stating the problem, the solution, costs versus returns, hazards, and a deployment plan — chosen on purpose, not because it is trendy.
* Cost-benefit analysis spans implementation, operational, and risk/compliance costs against efficiency, better decisions, and cost reduction; early ROI often looks poor before it pays off.
* Internal hosting gives control but high costs, while cloud hosting gives scalability but brings vendor lock-in, latency, and data residency compliance concerns.
* Vendors must be vetted for ethical alignment, explainability, and security, then managed through regularly reviewed service level agreements.
* The shared responsibility model (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS) defines who is liable, but the purchasing organization always owns user training, access controls, and data governance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should an AI business case include?
A documented AI business case must state the exact problem the AI is supposed to solve, explain how the technology will solve it, include a financial breakdown of expected costs versus anticipated returns, identify potential hazards and mitigation plans, and provide a step-by-step deployment plan. This ensures technology is chosen on purpose, not just because it is popular.

### What are the trade-offs between internal hosting and cloud hosting for AI?
Internal hosting provides ultimate control, customization, and data security, but you are limited by your own hardware, face high electricity costs, and must hire technicians. Cloud hosting offers flexibility, scalability, and low initial costs, but brings loss of physical control, vendor lock-in, network latency, and data residency compliance concerns over where servers are located.

### What is the shared responsibility model in AI?
The shared responsibility model defines which tasks belong to your organization, which belong to the vendor, and where they overlap. With Infrastructure as a Service you are responsible for most tasks, Platform as a Service shares responsibility, and Software as a Service shifts most work to the vendor. However, you always remain responsible for user training, access controls, and governing your own data.

### What are the main risks of buying off-the-shelf AI solutions?
Off-the-shelf AI solutions introduce three major risks. Accountability becomes blurred if no one documents who maintains the system, ethical concerns arise over who is liable when a pre-built algorithm makes a biased decision, and data security weakens because sharing private data creates new attack vectors for hackers.
